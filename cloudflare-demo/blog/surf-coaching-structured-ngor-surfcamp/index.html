Who is this for?
Written by
Luca Ferretti
ISA Surf Coach
An ISA-certified surf coach from Sardinia, Luca specializes in video analysis and progression methodology. With 12 years of professional coaching across Europe and West Africa, he joined Ngor Surfcamp's team in 2022.

Good surf coaching is not a loud whistle from the beach or a generic list of things to “improve.” At Ngor Surfcamp Teranga, coaching is structured around one simple idea: every surfer progresses faster when they understand what they are trying to do, why it matters on a specific wave, and how to repeat it under pressure.

That matters even more on Ngor Island, where the waves have personality. Ngor Right can be fast, hollow and technical over reef. Ngor Left offers a longer, more forgiving wall where timing, trim and flow become easier to feel. A proper coaching system here has to do more than motivate. It has to translate these waves into clear, usable steps.

Why structure matters more than motivation

A lot of surfers arrive in Senegal with the same frustration. They surf regularly at home, they are fit enough, and they know the theory in broad terms, but their surfing still feels inconsistent. One session everything clicks; the next day they are late on take-offs, stuck in the flats, or rushing turns.

That inconsistency is usually not about courage or commitment. It is about missing links in the learning chain.

At Ngor Surfcamp Teranga, the coaching methodology is built to close those links. Each day combines observation, a focused objective, the right wave choice, in-water guidance, and a feedback loop afterward. Instead of trying to fix ten things at once, the coaches narrow the session to one or two priorities that actually move your surfing forward.

For a beginner, that might mean standing up with balance and building a repeatable take-off. For an intermediate surfer, it could be learning to set the rail early and generate speed instead of pumping without purpose. For an advanced surfer, it may be positioning on the reef, reading the section at Ngor Right, and refining line choice for a tighter barrel approach.

The fastest progression happens when one clear correction is repeated enough times to become automatic.

This is why surf coaching ngor surfcamp is not treated as a bonus extra. It is woven into the rhythm of the stay: before the surf, during the surf, and after the surf.

TIP: Bring a small notebook or use your phone after each session; writing down one successful feeling and one mistake makes coach feedback stick far better by the next paddle-out.

The daily coaching rhythm: before, during and after the surf

The structure starts before anyone enters the water. Conditions around Ngor Island change with tide, swell angle, wind and crowd rhythm, so the first coaching job is wave selection. A good coach does not send every level to the same peak. They match the surfer to the window that gives the highest chance of useful repetition.

That often begins with a briefing at camp. The team talks through the forecast, the tide push, what Ngor Right or Ngor Left is likely to do, and what that means for positioning. If the wave is steeper and more technical, the objective might be commitment and early hands placement on take-off. If the wall is softer and longer, the goal may be trimming high, linking sections and using the face better.

The theory component is practical, not abstract. Coaches explain what surfers should notice as they paddle out: where the peak shifts, how the channel works, which landmarks help line-up, and how the wave changes from first section to inside.

Then comes water time, where the day’s key point is reinforced in real conditions. The coach watches wave choice, paddle timing, pop-up mechanics, stance width, shoulder alignment, where the eyes are looking, and whether the surfer’s line matches the section in front of them.

Afterward, the feedback loop begins. Sometimes that feedback is immediate on the beach or in the boat transfer back. Sometimes it is delivered in a more detailed debrief, especially when video analysis is part of the package. Either way, the idea is the same: identify what happened, explain why it happened, and set the next correction.

This structure is one reason many guests who book a week expecting “a nice surf trip” end up making more progress than they have in months at home. The environment is immersive, but the coaching process stays grounded and specific.

Theory sessions: turning wave knowledge into usable decisions

Theory sessions are often underestimated because they do not look dramatic. But in practice, they save weeks of trial and error.

At Ngor Surfcamp Teranga, theory is not a lecture for the sake of appearing professional. It is where surfers learn to decode the waves they are about to ride. On a reef setup like Ngor, that matters enormously. The difference between a clean entry and a rushed take-off is often decided before the wave even arrives.

For newer surfers, theory usually begins with essentials: board handling, paddling posture, where to place the hands in the pop-up, and how to angle the take-off. Coaches break down common mistakes in plain language. Too far back on the board? You stall. Too narrow in stance? You wobble. Looking down instead of down the line? Your body follows your head and the ride dies early.

For intermediate surfers, theory sessions move toward timing, line and intention. What part of the face are you aiming for after take-off? Are you dropping to the bottom automatically when the better line is higher? Are you trying to turn before you have created speed? These are the kinds of details that unlock real surfing rather than just successful standing.

Advanced surfers usually use theory to sharpen tactical understanding. On Ngor Right, the wave can offer a short window where hesitation costs the whole ride. Coaches talk through entry angle, compression, where to project on the reef, and how to read the section if the barrel line is available. This is not fantasy coaching. It is practical preparation for a wave that rewards decisiveness.

Theory sessions also create a shared language between coach and surfer. That matters once you are in the water. If a coach later says “set your line earlier” or “open your shoulders less on the take-off,” you already know exactly what adjustment is being requested.

Surfing improves fastest when the body learns what the mind can already recognize.

In-water coaching: where corrections become habits

There is a reason in-water coaching remains the core of the system. Surfing is highly sensory. You can understand a movement intellectually and still fail to reproduce it when the wave stands up. The ocean compresses decision-making. A coach’s role is to narrow that chaos.

At Ngor Surfcamp Teranga, in-water coaching is not random cheering from the shoulder. The coaches watch sequences: paddle commitment, timing of the last strokes, whether the surfer hesitates, how quickly they set the rail, and whether they surf the wave they caught or the wave they wished they had caught.

For beginners, in-water guidance often focuses on wave selection and consistency. Many novices do not need more courage; they need cleaner repetition on the right waves. The coaches help them identify which peaks offer manageable entries and enough wall to practice standing, trimming and staying relaxed. The point is to replace survival mode with familiarity.

For intermediates, the emphasis usually shifts from simply catching waves to using them better. This is where a lot of plateaus happen. A surfer can pop up reliably but still lose speed, flatten the board, or end every wave with a rushed cutback attempt. Coaches use the conditions at Ngor Left especially well here, because its longer wall gives enough time to feel what proper trim and speed generation should feel like.

For advanced surfers, in-water coaching becomes more tactical and more precise. Positioning a metre deeper can change the entire ride. So can the timing of the bottom turn, the line through a racing section, or the decision to stay high and make the tube line instead of dropping too low. At Ngor Right, those refinements matter.

The best coaching moments in the water are often small. A surfer shifts two inches forward when paddling and suddenly enters earlier. Another stops looking at the lip and starts looking through the section, and the whole line cleans up. These are not glamorous breakthroughs, but they are the ones that last.

The feedback loop: what video analysis adds

Many surfers know how they think they surf. Video shows how they actually surf. The difference between the two can be uncomfortable, but it is also where progress accelerates.

Video analysis at the camp is especially useful because it turns fleeting sensations into something concrete. Maybe the wave felt fast, but the clip shows your take-off was late because you paused your paddling. Maybe you thought your stance was compact, but the footage reveals you are standing too tall through every bottom turn. Maybe your line looked committed from your perspective, but on camera you can see the section was offering more space than you used.

The key is how the review is handled. Good video feedback does not become a list of faults. It isolates the most important patterns and connects them to a simple next step. The coaches do not overload surfers with technical jargon. They show the issue, explain its consequence, and give a correction to test in the next session.

Skill progression: from pop-up to turns to barrels

One of the strengths of the coaching structure is that progression follows a logical ladder. The camp welcomes all levels, but the method respects the order in which surfing skills actually build on each other.

Stage 1: foundations and repeatability

The first stage is about making the basics repeatable under pressure. That includes paddling efficiently, choosing suitable waves, popping up without hesitation, finding a balanced stance, and riding with enough control to stay relaxed.

This stage matters more than many surfers want to admit. If the take-off is unreliable, everything that follows is fragile. A surfer cannot learn meaningful turns if half their energy is spent just standing up.

At Ngor, coaches often use mellower windows or sections of Ngor Left for this phase. The aim is not to avoid challenge forever. It is to create enough successful repetition that the basics stop consuming all of the surfer’s attention.

Stage 2: trim, line and speed

Once the take-off is more dependable, the next step is learning how to travel on the wave with intention. This means trimming in the right part of the face, understanding when to stay high, and using body position to keep speed rather than constantly chasing it.

This is where a lot of intermediate surfers make their first real leap. They realize surfing is not just reacting. It is anticipating the section and placing the board where the wave gives energy back.

Stage 3: bottom turns and top turns

Turning becomes meaningful only when the surfer has speed and a clear line. Coaches begin by refining the setup: where the surfer looks, how they compress, where they place weight through the bottom turn, and whether they release the turn with enough direction to meet the lip or high line.

On longer, cleaner walls, this can be developed progressively. First, the aim is not spray or aggression. It is control. Can the surfer complete a bottom turn that actually sets up the next section? Can they perform a top turn without losing all momentum?

Stage 4: linking manoeuvres

The next progression is combination surfing. Instead of isolated moves, the surfer starts to connect take-off, trim, bottom turn, top turn, cutback or rebound into one flowing line. Timing becomes more important than effort.

This is where surfers begin to look more composed. The ride stops feeling like a sequence of emergencies and starts feeling like a plan.

Stage 5: advanced positioning and barrel approach

Only when those earlier layers are reasonably solid does advanced coaching on hollow waves become truly productive. Ngor Right can offer barrel sections, but coaches do not sell the fantasy of “tube coaching” to surfers who still need work on entry, line or speed control. The structure remains honest.

For advanced surfers who are ready, the coaching shifts to precision: deeper positioning, earlier commitment, staying compact, reading the shoulder versus the pitching lip, and deciding whether to race, stall, or project.

Key Takeaways
  • SUMMARY:
  • Strong take-offs create the platform for every later skill
  • Speed and line come before stylish turns
  • Barrel coaching only works when positioning and control are already solid
The barrel is not a separate skill. It is the result of doing several earlier skills at the right time on the right section.

How coaches adapt to each surfer, not just each level

Level labels are useful, but they can also be misleading. Two intermediates may need completely different coaching. One might be athletic and fearless but too rushed in decision-making. Another may read waves well yet hesitate at take-off. Both say they want to “improve turns,” but the real work starts in different places.

That is why the coaching at Ngor Surfcamp Teranga adapts to the individual, not only the category.

The first layer of adaptation is physical. Board size, fitness, mobility, paddling strength and fatigue all influence what is realistic in a session. A coach may shorten the objective for a tired surfer instead of insisting on an ambitious progression point that the body cannot support that day.

The second layer is psychological. Some surfers need more commitment. Others need more calm. Some benefit from technical detail. Others surf better with one simple image in mind. A good coach reads this quickly.

The third layer is wave matching. Not every surfer improves fastest in the same conditions. One person may unlock turns on a softer wall. Another may clean up their take-off precisely because a steeper wave forces better habits. Ngor Left and Ngor Right give the coaching team useful range to make these decisions.

This personalised approach is one reason the camp works well for mixed groups too. Friends or couples do not need identical goals for the trip to be successful. One surfer can focus on confidence and basics while another works on performance details, and both still share the same island experience.

Why Ngor Island is such a good classroom

Some surf destinations are beautiful but poor for learning because the logistics are stressful or the conditions change too wildly from one session to the next. Ngor Island is different. The short transfer from the mainland, the proximity of the breaks, and the balance between a more forgiving left and a more technical right create a very practical coaching environment.

You are not losing half the day to long road missions. You are close to the waves, close to the camp, and close to the debrief space. That matters. Better coaching often comes from tighter rhythms, not longer adventures.

The camp itself supports that rhythm. Accommodation options include private rooms, shared rooms and dorms, and the package includes breakfast and dinner, surf guiding, theory sessions and access to the pool. That sounds simple, but in coaching terms it helps keep surfers rested, fed and ready to process information. Progress suffers when people are constantly scattered or exhausted.

Being FSS licensed also matters. It signals that the surf operation is not improvised. There is a framework, local legitimacy and a commitment to doing things properly in Senegal’s surf scene.

For most surfers, the prime season from November to April offers the best combination of swell and consistency. This is when the coaching structure can be used to its full potential. During the flatter off-season from May to October, the value shifts more toward lifestyle and lower-intensity sessions where possible.

How to get the most from coaching during your stay

Even the best coaching system works better when the surfer meets it halfway. The guests who improve most are usually not the most talented. They are the most coachable.

That means arriving with honesty. If your pop-up falls apart under pressure, say so. If you are scared of steeper take-offs, admit it. If your main problem is fitness, not technique, that is useful information too. Coaches can work with reality; they cannot work with ego.

It also helps to commit to the process for several sessions instead of expecting instant transformation. Surfing is not learned in straight lines. One day you will feel the correction clearly. The next day the ocean will humble you again. That does not mean the method is failing. It usually means the new habit is still settling.

Use the camp’s extras strategically. Board rental can help if your usual board is holding you back or not suited to the conditions. Wetsuit rental can make longer sessions more comfortable in cooler periods. Video analysis is especially worth prioritising if you tend to be a visual learner.

Most importantly, resist the urge to chase every advanced move too early. The surfers who make the cleanest long-term progress are usually the ones who accept the order of learning. Better take-off first. Better line second. Better turns third. Hollow-wave performance last.

What this coaching structure feels like in practice

By the middle of a good week, most surfers notice a shift. The waves have not become easier, but they start to feel more readable. The paddle-out is less chaotic. The take-off has more intention. A missed section no longer feels random; it has a reason. That awareness is one of the clearest signs that coaching is working.

You are no longer just collecting rides. You are building understanding.

That is the real value of surf coaching ngor surfcamp. It is not about turning every guest into a high-performance surfer in a few days. It is about creating a structure in which each surfer, at their own level, can identify the next meaningful step and actually feel it in the water.

On Ngor Island, with the Atlantic moving over volcanic reef and the rhythm of the camp keeping everything close and focused, that process feels natural. You surf, review, adjust, eat well, rest, and go again. Simple in theory, powerful in practice.

When coaching is done properly, confidence is no longer blind optimism. It is evidence. You have seen the mistake, understood it, corrected it, and repeated the better version enough times that it begins to belong to you.
